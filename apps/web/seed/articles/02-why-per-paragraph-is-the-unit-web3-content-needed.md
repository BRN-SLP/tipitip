# Why per-paragraph is the unit Web3 content needed

Substack put a tip button at the bottom of the post. Patreon put a tip button on the writer. Twitter put a tip button on the tweet. Each iteration moved the unit closer to the work, and each iteration was bottlenecked by the price of a payment.

A blog post is too large a unit. By the time you've finished reading, the strongest thing in the essay is already two paragraphs behind you, and the bottom button asks you to average. Average tipping is not a payment for a paragraph; it is a guilt offering for the writer.

A monthly subscription is the opposite problem. It detaches the act of paying from the act of reading. You support someone, and then on the days they write something thin you feel cheated, and on the days they write something brilliant the brilliance is invisible to them — already paid for, already filed.

The tweet, in fairness, is the right size. But a tweet is also a unit that cannot hold an argument. You cannot write the case for free speech in 280 characters; you can only signal that you have one. The paragraph is the smallest unit that holds a complete idea.

The economic claim is simple: micropayments only work when the friction is invisible and the unit is small. A 5-cent tip on a 5-paragraph post is a single dollar that the writer can taste, paragraph by paragraph, instead of a single dollar that the writer can only count.

Celo is the first L1 where the friction is invisible and the unit can be small. cUSD is stable, gas is sub-cent, and MiniPay puts a wallet in the pocket of every Opera Mini user in Africa by default. The rail finally matches the unit. The only thing left to build was the button.
