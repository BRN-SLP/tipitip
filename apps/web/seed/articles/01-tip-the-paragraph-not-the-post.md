# Tip the paragraph, not the post

A book has chapters. A chapter has paragraphs. A paragraph has one idea. So why does the tip jar sit at the bottom of the page, where the reader has to remember the whole thing before deciding whether the whole thing earned a coin?

When tipping is per-article, the reader rewards the headline, not the writing. The best paragraph in a mediocre essay never gets paid. The throwaway line in a great essay gets paid twice. The unit is wrong, and so the economy on top of it is wrong.

Every paragraph here has a small heart underneath it. Tap once, and a fraction of a cent moves from your wallet to mine. There is no signup, no platform middle-tax, no monthly subscription that turns reading into a chore. There is a coin, and there are the specific words that earned it.

For writers, this changes what you optimize for. You stop chasing the 90-second engagement metric. You start writing paragraphs that have to earn their place — knowing that if the third one is the only one anyone tips, that is data about the third one, not a verdict on the essay.

For readers, this gives you a vocabulary you didn't have. You can love half an essay. You can disagree with the thesis and pay for the imagery. You can hand a coin to the one sentence that stayed with you on the walk home, and the writer will see, exactly, which sentence it was.

Tap the heart under this paragraph if it convinced you. If not, that's the point — the next paragraph is somebody else's, and so is the one after that, and the unit was always the paragraph, we just didn't have the rails to act like it.
